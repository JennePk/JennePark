Why Packing Matters More Than You Think

When I first started planning my trip, I thought packing would be the easiest part.

Just bring clothes, right?

Wrong.

Japan is an amazing country, but it’s also very different from Europe in small, practical ways. Things you take for granted here might not exist there—or work differently.

For example:

  • You won’t always find trash bins
  • Some places only accept cash
  • Language can be a barrier in unexpected situations
  • You’ll walk A LOT (like… a lot)

So packing smart is not just about comfort. It’s about avoiding stress, saving time, and feeling confident when you’re alone in a new country. Everything I’m about to share comes from research, advice, and imagining myself in real situations

What’s in My Bag — Essentials

1.Passport + Copies

This is obvious, but also easy to underestimate.

In Japan, you need your passport for identification in many situations. Some hotels require it. Tax-free shopping requires it. And in general, you should always have it with you. But here’s the thing: losing your passport abroad is a nightmare.

So my tip is simple:

  • Keep your passport safe in your bag
  • Take photos of it and store them on your phone
  • Have a printed copy in a separate place

This way, even in a worst-case scenario, you’re not completely stuck.

2.Travel Insurance Card

Before planning anything fun, I realized something important: Healthcare abroad can be expensive—and complicated. Japan has excellent healthcare, but as a foreigner, you need proper insurance. Also, if you’re from Europe like me, the European Health Insurance Card (EHIC) doesn’t fully cover Japan.

So I made sure to:

  • Get proper travel insurance
  • Keep the insurance card accessible
  • Save emergency contact numbers on my phone

It’s one of those things you hope you’ll never need, but you’ll be very glad to have it if something happens.

3.IC Card (Suica / Pasmo)

This is one of the smartest things you can get for Japan.

An IC card is basically a prepaid card you use for: Trains, subways, Buses and even convenience stores and vending machines Instead of buying tickets every time, you just tap and go.

From what I’ve learned, this saves: Time, stress and confusion (especially when you don’t speak Japanese) My plan:👉 Get one at the airport and load money immediately.

4.Portable WiFi / SIM

This is probably one of the most important items for a solo traveler. Imagine being in Tokyo, not understanding the signs, not speaking the language… and having no internet. No Google Maps, No translator, No way to check train routes. That’s a nightmare scenario.

So I decided: 👉 I will get internet BEFORE arriving in Japan.

Options:

  • Portable WiFi device
  • SIM card or eSIM

Personally, I prefer something ready as soon as I land. Because when you’re alone, being connected = feeling safe.

5. Power Bank (20,000mAh)

If your phone dies, everything becomes harder.

And in Japan, you’ll use your phone constantly:

  • Navigation
  • Translation
  • Photos
  • Payments (sometimes)

Also, days can be long—especially when exploring cities. So I chose a high-capacity power bank (around 20,000mAh). My rule: 👉 Charge it every night, no exceptions.

6. Cash in Yen

This surprised me a lot. Japan is modern… but still very cash-based. Many places (especially small restaurants or local shops) don’t accept cards. So I’ll make sure to: Carry cash at all times and Withdraw money from ATMs (7-Eleven is often recommended). It feels old-school, but it’s part of the experience.

7. Universal Adapter

This is something people often forget. Japan uses Type A plugs (like in the US), not European plugs. So your charger won’t fit. Simple solution: 👉 Bring a universal adapter. Without it, your devices are useless.

8. Reusable Bag (Tote)

This is a small detail—but very important in Japan. Plastic bags are not always free. Sometimes they are not even available. Also, Japan has a strong eco-friendly culture.

So bringing your own reusable bag is:

  • Practical
  • Respectful
  • Convenient (especially for shopping)

9. Small Hand Towel

This one is very “Japan-specific”. In many public restrooms, you won’t find paper towels. Instead, people carry small towels. At first, I thought it was strange… but now I actually like the idea. It’s simple and eco-friendly.

10. Basic Medical Kit

Being sick abroad is already stressful. Being sick abroad and not speaking the language? Even worse.

So I’m preparing a small kit with:

  • Paracetamol
  • Plasters
  • Basic medicine I trust

Because finding the exact equivalent in Japan might not be easy.

11. Comfortable Trainers

This might be the MOST important item of all. Everyone says the same thing: 👉 You will walk 10,000 to 20,000 steps per day. No joke. So forget about style over comfort. Good shoes = better experience. Bad shoes = pain, stress, ruined days. Simple as that.
