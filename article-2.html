Where to go? 7-night Japan itinerary

A beginner-friendly route through Tokyo, Kyoto, Osaka & more

By Jenne PK · April 2026 · 10 min read

When I started planning my trip to Japan, I had no idea where to go. Tokyo, Kyoto, Osaka… everything looked amazing but overwhelming.

So I built a simple route — realistic, balanced, and beginner-friendly. This is the itinerary I’m personally using for my solo trip.

7-night recommended route

The moment I landed in Japan, everything felt unreal. I arrived at Narita Airport after a long flight, tired but excited. It was my first time in Japan — and honestly, I didn’t really know what to expect.

But stepping into Tokyo changed everything. The trains were silent, the signs were everywhere, and even the air felt different. That’s when I realized: this trip is not just about sightseeing — it’s about learning how to move in a completely new world.

Nights 1–2: Tokyo — your first shock (in a good way)

Tokyo is usually your first real experience of Japan. It’s overwhelming at first — but in the best possible way.

Areas like Shinjuku, Harajuku, and Akihabara give you completely different versions of the city: neon chaos, fashion culture, anime energy, and ultra-modern tech streets.

TeamLab Borderless is also a must-see experience. It’s not just an exhibition — it feels like stepping inside a digital dream.

👉 Tip: Don’t try to do everything on your first day. Jet lag is real, and Tokyo is bigger than it looks. Just walk, observe, and adapt slowly.

Night 3: Hakone — slow down and breathe

After Tokyo’s intensity, Hakone feels like a reset button. This is where you experience Japan’s natural side: mountains, onsen (hot springs), and sometimes even Mt Fuji views.

Staying in a ryokan here is one of the most authentic experiences in Japan. Sleeping on tatami floors, wearing yukata, and having a traditional dinner changes your pace completely.

👉 Tip: Book ryokan stays early. They are limited, and the best ones get full weeks (or months) in advance.

Nights 4–5: Kyoto — where Japan feels timeless

Kyoto is often the emotional highlight of the trip. It feels like stepping into another century.

Walking through Fushimi Inari at dawn, seeing thousands of red torii gates, is unforgettable. It’s also much better early in the morning — before the crowds arrive.

Arashiyama Bamboo Forest is another iconic spot, but timing is everything: go early (around 6–7am) if you want to feel the atmosphere.

👉 Tip: Kyoto is not about rushing. It’s about slowing down and letting the city guide you.

Night 6: Nara — simplicity and nature

Nara is a day trip that feels surprisingly peaceful. The famous deer park is not just cute — it reflects how nature and city coexist here.

You’ll also find ancient temples like Todai-ji, home to one of the largest Buddha statues in Japan.

👉 Tip: Be careful with the deer — they are friendly, but they expect food. Don’t tease them or hold snacks too long.

Night 7: Osaka — food, energy, and nightlife

Osaka feels completely different again. It’s louder, more relaxed, and deeply focused on food culture.

Dotonbori is the heart of the city — neon lights, street food stalls, and constant movement everywhere.

This is the perfect place to end your trip: eat everything you can, walk without plans, and enjoy the atmosphere.

🎬 Ghibli & Animation Spots (why these places feel so special)

Many travelers go to Japan because of anime — but what surprises most people is that these places don’t feel like “tourist attractions”. They feel alive.

Ghibli Museum (Tokyo)

The Ghibli Museum in Mitaka is not a normal museum. It feels like entering the creative world of Studio Ghibli itself.

👉 Important: You must book tickets in advance — usually around 1 month before. Tickets are limited because they want to preserve the experience and avoid overcrowding.

Why it matters: It’s not just about seeing art — it’s about experiencing how those worlds were imagined.

Fushimi Inari (Kyoto)

Walking through thousands of torii gates feels like entering a different dimension. Many people associate it with Spirited Away vibes.

👉 Go early morning or late evening — otherwise it becomes extremely crowded.

Arashiyama Bamboo Grove

This place feels like Princess Mononoke in real life. But again — timing matters.

👉 Early morning is the only way to experience its quiet atmosphere.

Nara Deer Park

People often compare this to Ghibli-like nature scenes. Deer move freely, and the environment feels almost surreal.

teamLab Borderless

This is a fully immersive digital art experience. There is no fixed path — you walk and the art reacts to you.

👉 It’s popular, so booking in advance is strongly recommended.

🏯 Traditional Experiences — more than just activities

These experiences are not just “things to do”. They are a way to understand how Japan feels from the inside.

Onsen (hot spring bath)

Onsen are natural hot springs found across Japan, especially in places like Hakone. It’s a deeply relaxing cultural experience.

👉 Watch out: Tattoos are sometimes restricted in certain onsen.

Ryokan stay

A ryokan is a traditional Japanese inn. Everything slows down here — food, sleep, atmosphere.

👉 Book early — especially in Hakone or Kyoto regions.

Tea ceremony

Matcha tea ceremonies are not about drinking tea quickly. They are about precision, silence, and respect.

Kimono walk

Renting a kimono in Kyoto and walking through Gion gives you a completely different feeling of the city.

Sumo practice

Watching sumo morning practice in Tokyo is rare but powerful. It gives you a real look into discipline and tradition.
