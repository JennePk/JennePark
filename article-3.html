🏨 Sleeping on a Tiny Budget: What Are Your Real Options?

Japan isn't as expensive as people think — if you plan smart. Here are the accommodation options I researched while trying to stay under €50 per night.

Capsule Hotels — The Experience You've Seen Online

Capsule hotels are one of Japan's most iconic budget experiences. They look futuristic, affordable and uniquely Japanese. A capsule is essentially a sleeping pod containing a mattress, power outlet, reading light and privacy curtain. Bathrooms and toilets are usually shared. They're typically clean and well maintained, but privacy is limited.

Cost: Around ¥2,500–¥4,500 per night (€15–€30).

Best for: Solo travelers looking for a cheap and memorable experience.

👉 Tip: Look for business capsule hotels or pod hotels. They tend to be quieter and cleaner.

Airbnb in Japan — Does It Actually Work?

Yes, but with a few caveats.

Japan has stricter short-term rental laws than many countries, but legal Airbnb options still exist. In Tokyo and Osaka, private apartments can often be found for €40–€70 per night.

Pros: More space, kitchen access, privacy and local neighborhoods. Cons: Fewer listings and some properties are far from stations.

👉 Always check that the host displays a registration number.

Dorm Hostels — The Social Option

Japan has some of the best hostels in the world. Many feature modern minimalist design, communal spaces and excellent cleanliness.

  • €15–€30 per night
  • Great for meeting travelers
  • Central locations
  • Shared facilities and limited privacy

👉 Aim for ratings above 9/10 when booking.

Budget Business Hotels — My Favorite Option

For a first trip, budget business hotels seem like the best balance.

Chains such as Toyoko Inn, APA Hotel and Super Hotel often offer:

  • Private rooms
  • Private bathrooms
  • Good station locations
  • Reliable WiFi

Prices are usually around ¥5,000–¥8,000 per night (€30–€50).

👉 Book Kyoto accommodation as early as possible.

🗓️ My 7-Day Itinerary : Day by Day

Day 1 : Landing at Narita

Arrive. Get your bearings. Don't try to conquer Tokyo immediately. First priority: pick up a Suica or Pasmo IC card. It works on most trains, metros and buses. After checking in, explore your local area and have an easy first evening.

Stay: Tokyo (2 nights)

Day 2 — Tokyo

Morning: Asakusa and Senso-ji Temple. Afternoon: Harajuku and Shibuya Crossing. Evening: Shinjuku, ramen and city views.

👉 Book Ghibli Museum tickets before your trip if it's on your list.

Day 3 — Hakone<

Escape the city and slow down. Check into a ryokan, visit the Open Air Museum and enjoy an onsen in the evening.

Stay: Hakone (1 night)

Days 4–5 — Kyoto

Kyoto is the emotional heart of the trip. Day 4: Fushimi Inari and Gion. Day 5: Arashiyama Bamboo Grove, tea ceremony and slow exploration.

Stay: Kyoto (2 nights)

Day 6 — Nara

Visit Todai-ji Temple and Nara Deer Park. Then continue to Osaka for the evening.

Stay: Osaka (1 night)

Day 7 — Osaka

Food, neon lights and a relaxed final day. Explore Dotonbori, Osaka Castle and Shinsekai. Eat everything. Regret nothing.

🌸 Summer Festival Bonus

If you're visiting during summer, consider planning around:

  • Gion Matsuri (Kyoto)
  • Tenjin Matsuri (Osaka)
  • Sumida River Fireworks (Tokyo)

👉 Book accommodation months ahead during festival season.

❌ My Mistakes — So You Don't Have To Make Them

Mistake #1: Too Many Cities

I originally planned six cities in seven days. It looked exciting but would have been exhausting.

Fix: Focus on fewer destinations.

Mistake #2: Not Thinking About Shinkansen Costs

Train travel adds up quickly. Research rail passes and transportation costs before finalizing your route.

Mistake #3: Trusting One App for Everything<

Use multiple transit tools and always double-check station exits.

Mistake #4: Assuming Cards Work Everywhere

Cash is still important in Japan.

Mistake #5: Waiting Too Long To Book Kyoto

Kyoto accommodation disappears surprisingly fast.

Mistake #6: Underestimating Jet Lag

Day 1 should be easy. Save major sightseeing for Day 2.
