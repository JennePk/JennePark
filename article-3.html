Japan Planning: Budget Hotels, My Full 7-Day Itinerary & The Mistakes I Almost Made

Part 3 of my Japan series — where the real planning begins

By Jenne PK · June 2026 · 11 min read

When I published my first article about where to go in Japan, I got a lot of messages asking the same thing:

"Okay, but how do you actually plan it?"

Budget. Accommodation. Real day by day schedules. And most importantly — the mistakes you need to avoid before you even buy your plane ticket.

So here's Part 3. The planning process. The honest version.

🏨 Sleeping on a Tiny Budget: What Are Your Real Options?

Let me be clear: Japan is not as expensive as people think, if you plan smart. Here's what I found when I started researching accommodation for under €50/night.

Capsule Hotels. The Experience You've Seen in Videos

Capsule hotels are probably the most iconic budget option in Japan. You've seen the photos: tiny pods stacked on top of each other, neon lighting, futuristic vibes. But what's the reality for a beginner?

What you get: A capsule is essentially a sleeping pod. It's usually around 2m long and 1m wide. Inside, you'll find a small mattress, a light, a power outlet, sometimes a small TV, and a privacy curtain or sliding door. That's it. That's your room.

Do you have a private toilet? No. Almost never. Capsule hotels work on a shared facility model. Toilets, showers, and sinks are communal, separated by gender. The bathrooms are usually clean and well-maintained (this is Japan, after all), but you share them with every other guest on your floor. If privacy is important to you, this is something to think about seriously.

Cost:Around ¥2,500–¥4,500 per night (roughly €15–€30). One of the cheapest options in any major Japanese city.

Best for: Solo travelers who don't mind minimalism, want a uniquely Japanese experience, and are okay with shared spaces.

Not ideal for: People with claustrophobia, light sleepers (some capsules have thin curtains, not full doors), or anyone who needs quiet morning routines.

👉 Tip: Search for capsule hotels labelled "business capsule" or "pod hotel", they tend to be cleaner and quieter than the ultra-cheap ones. Popular chains include First Cabin and 9 Hours.

Airbnb in Japan. Does It Actually Work?

Short answer: yes, but with asterisks.

Japan has fairly strict short-term rental laws. After regulations passed in 2018, many Airbnb listings were removed. But options still exist, you just need to look carefully.

What to expect: Airbnb in Japan is often a small private apartment or a room in someone's home. In Tokyo and Osaka especially, you can find clean, well-located apartments for €40–€70/night, which, split with a travel partner, becomes very affordable.

Pros: More space, a kitchen (huge for saving on food), privacy, local neighborhood vibe.

Cons: Fewer options than in Europe. Some listings are far from train stations. You need to verify the host is legally registered (look for the registration number in the listing, it's required by law).

👉 Tip:Filter by "entire place" if you want full privacy. Read reviews carefully — Japanese Airbnb hosts tend to be extremely detail-oriented in their communication, which is actually a good sign.

Dorm Hostels. The Social Option

Japan has an excellent hostel scene, especially in Tokyo, Kyoto, and Osaka. Many hostels here are actually beautiful, think minimalist design, wooden interiors, common areas with tea and tatami corners.

Pros:

  • Very cheap: €15–€30/night for a dorm bed
  • Great for meeting other solo travelers/li>
  • Often in great central locations
  • Common areas, events, and local tip boards
  • Many have private lockers and reading lights

Cons:

  • Shared dorms (usually 6–10 people) mean noise, early risers, late arrivals
  • Very limited personal space
  • No privacy, you'll be changing, sleeping, and waking up around strangers
  • Bathrooms are shared (similar to capsule hotels)

Best hostel areas to look in: Asakusa (Tokyo), Gion area (Kyoto), Namba (Osaka)

👉 Tip: Look for hostels rated 9+ on Booking.com or Hostelworld. Japanese hostels take cleanliness and service seriously, a high rating usually means a genuinely good experience.

Budget Business Hotels. The Underrated Sweet Spot

This is personally my favorite option for a first trip to Japan.

Budget business hotels like Toyoko Inn, TAPA Hotel, and TSuper Hotel offer private rooms with private bathrooms for around ¥5,000–¥8,000/night (€30–€50). They're not glamorous. But they're clean, well located near train stations, and give you the privacy of a normal hotel at hostel prices.

Pros:

  • Private room + private bathroom (no shared facilities)
  • Train station locations (extremely convenient in Japan)
  • Free breakfast at many Toyoko Inn locations
  • Reliable WiFi, AC, everything you need

Cons:

  • Rooms are tiny (but in Japan, that's normal)
  • Not much personality, very functional

👉 Tip: Toyoko Inn is my top recommendation for first-timers. Book 2–3 months in advance for best prices, especially in Kyoto which books up fast.

🗓️ My 7-Day Itinerary. Day by Day, Beginner Edition

Told as I imagined it, and then as I planned it.

Day 1: Landing at Narita. Don't Panic.

You land at Narita Airport. You're tired from a 10–14 hour flight. Everything is in Japanese. And somehow, the airport is the cleanest place you've ever been in your life.

First thing to do: Get your IC card (Suica or Pasmo) at the airport. This rechargeable card works on almost every train, metro, and bus in Japan. It will save your life. Do not skip this step.

How to get to Tokyo: Take the Narita Express (N'EX) directly to Shinjuku or Tokyo Station about 60–90 minutes, around ¥3,000. Or take the cheaper Keisei Skyliner to Ueno (¥2,520, 40 min). Both are fine.

Day 1 plan: Don't try to do too much. Arrive, check in, walk around your neighborhood. Eat something close by a convenience store onigiri from 7-Eleven counts as a great first meal in Japan, honestly. The combinis here are genuinely good.

Stay: Tokyo (Shinjuku or Asakusa area) Nights in Tokyo: 2 nights

Day 2 : Tokyo) Let the City Hit You

Today is your full first day. Tokyo is enormous, don't fight it, just pick 2–3 areas.

Morning: Asakusa + Senso-ji Temple. Go early (before 8am) to beat the crowds. The red lanterns and incense smoke in the morning light are genuinely magical.

Afternoon: Harajuku (Takeshita Street for fashion chaos) → Shibuya Crossing (just stand and watch it for 10 minutes — it's worth it).

Evening: Shinjuku. Walk through the neon streets around Kabukicho, grab dinner at a ramen shop, and if you have energy left, the view from the Tokyo Metropolitan Government Building is free and open some evenings.

👉Free tip: The Ghibli Museum in Mitaka requires advance booking (about 1 month ahead). Tickets are ¥1,000 and only available online through Lawson. If this is on your list, book before you even land.

Day 3: Hakone. Mountains, Onsen, Silence.

Take the Romancecar train from Shinjuku to Hakone (about 85 min, ¥2,470). The moment you arrive, the energy shifts. Mountains. Pine trees. No skyscrapers.

Highlight: Check into your ryokan. Even a budget ryokan (¥8,000–¥12,000/night) gives you the tatami floor, the yukata robe, and the onsen experience. This is worth splurging slightly on — it's one of the most unique nights of the trip.

Afternoon: Visit the Hakone Open Air Museum (¥1,600 entry) and enjoy beautiful sculptures surrounded by nature. Or take the ropeway toward a Mt. Fuji viewpoint (weather dependent, so check the forecast beforehand).

Night: Onsen at your ryokan. This is mandatory. Relax.

Stay: Hakone (1 night)

Days 4–5: Kyoto. The Emotional Heart of the Trip.

Take the Shinkansen from Odawara or Mishima to Kyoto (about 2 hours, ¥6,000–¥8,000). This is your first bullet train ride. Yes, it's as smooth as everyone says.

Day 4: Fushimi Inari + Gion

Wake up at 5am. Yes, 5am. Walk Fushimi Inari before the crowds arrive. The thousands of orange torii gates in the early morning mist, this is the moment most people say changed their trip. Entry is free, open 24/7.

AIn the afternoon, wander through Gion, Kyoto's geisha district. Stone alleys, wooden machiya houses, paper lanterns. If you rent a kimono for ¥2,000–¥3,500, you'll fit right in and feel the era shift around you.

Day 5: Arashiyama

Go early again (6–7am). The bamboo grove at Arashiyama is free to enter but extremely crowded by 9am. In the early morning, you might have it nearly to yourself.

After the bamboo grove: Tenryu-ji Garden (¥500 entry, the most beautiful zen garden I've ever seen in photos), a matcha tea ceremony in the area (¥1,500–¥2,500), and a slow walk along the Oi River.

Stay: Kyoto (2 nights)

Day 6: Nara. The Deer, the Buddha, the Quiet.

Nara is only 45 minutes from Kyoto by train (¥720 on the Kintetsu line), making it one of the easiest day trips in Japan.

Todai-ji Temple: One of the largest wooden buildings in the world, housing a giant bronze Buddha. Entry ¥600. Worth every yen.

Nara Deer Park: Free to wander. The deer are tame and will walk right up to you. Buy "shika senbei" (deer crackers) for ¥200 and prepare to be surrounded immediately.

👉 Warning: Don't hold the crackers in the air or try to hide them. The deer know. They will headbutt you.

Return to Osaka for the night. It's only 45 minutes away.

Stay: Osaka (1 night)

Day 7: Osaka. Eat Everything. Regret Nothing.

Your last full day. Osaka is the perfect ending because it asks nothing of you except to enjoy yourself.

Dotonbori: The neon canal strip. Takoyaki (octopus balls), okonomiyaki (savoury pancakes), kushikatsu (deep-fried skewers). This is the food capital of Japan. Eat lunch here. Then eat again. It's fine.

Osaka Castle: ¥600 entry. Surrounded by a beautiful park and perfect for a slow afternoon walk.

Evening: Explore Shinsekai for retro Osaka vibes, or simply return to Dotonbori and walk until midnight. Osaka stays alive late.

Stay: Osaka (1 night before flying home)

🌸 Summer Festival Bonus: Free Events Worth Planning Around

If you're traveling in July or August, Japan's summer festival season is something else entirely.

  • Gion Matsuri (Kyoto, July): One of Japan's most famous festivals. The main procession (Yamaboko Junko) takes place on July 17th. free to watch from the streets. Thousands of people in yukata, traditional floats, street food everywhere.
  • Tenjin Matsuri (Osaka, July 24–25): Boat processions, fireworks and a fantastic festival atmosphere along the river.
  • Sumida River Fireworks (Tokyo, late July): One of the biggest hanabi (fireworks) events in Japan. Free viewing from riverside parks, but go early to claim your spot.

👉Book accommodation 3–4 months ahead if your trip overlaps with any major matsuri. Prices spike and things sell out fast.

❌ My Mistakes: So You Don't Have To Make Them

This is the part I wish someone had told me before I started planning.

Mistake #1: Planning Too Many Cities in 7 Days

I originally had 6 cities in 7 days. Tokyo → Nikko → Hakone → Kyoto → Nara → Hiroshima → Osaka. It sounds amazing on paper. In reality, you spend half your trip on trains, you're exhausted by day 4, and you never go deep into any place.

Fix: 4 destinations maximum for 7 nights. Depth over breadth, always.

Mistake #2: Not Booking the Shinkansen in Advance

The bullet train is not cheap on-the-spot. I almost paid full price until I found out about the JR Pass, a tourist pass that gives you unlimited Shinkansen travel for 7, 14, or 21 days. The 7-day pass costs around ¥50,000 (≈€300) but if you're doing Tokyo → Hakone → Kyoto → Osaka with day trips, it pays for itself.

Important update: The JR Pass must be purchased before you arrive in Japan or at least activated at the airport. Don't wait until you're in the country.

Mistake #3: Assuming Google Maps Works Perfectly For Trains

Google Maps is good in Japan, but it doesn't always show the cheapest route or the local IC card price. Download Hyperdia or Japan Official Travel App for more accurate transit planning. And always check which exit of a station you need some Tokyo stations have 50+ exits and the wrong one can leave you 15 minutes from where you want to be.

Mistake #4: Forgetting That Most Restaurants Don't Take Cards

Japan is still surprisingly cash-oriented, especially in smaller restaurants, temples and markets. I almost went hungry on Day 2 because I only carried a Visa card.

Fix: Withdraw ¥30,000–¥50,000 in cash at the airport (7-Eleven ATMs accept foreign cards reliably). Keep cash on you at all times.

Mistake #5: Trying To Wing Accommodation In Kyoto

Kyoto has fewer hotel rooms than Tokyo, and it fills up, especially during sakura season (late March–April) and autumn foliage (November). I checked last minute and the options I wanted were completely gone.

Fix: Book Kyoto accommodation first, before anything else. It's the bottleneck of the whole trip.

Mistake #6: Underestimating Jet Lag

If you're flying from Europe, you're crossing 7–9 time zones. Your body will be confused for 2–3 days. I originally planned big activities for Day 1 evening. That was naive.

Fix: Keep Day 1 light. Walk, eat, sleep. Start the real itinerary on Day 2.
